La Música
Hecha En Chicago

The Rise of Chicago's Latin DIY Music Scene

By Victoria Cabales, Andres Correa, Anna White, and Courtney Zhu

On the Friday eve before Halloween, Kimberly Lopez and Lupe Arrieta wait in the 20-person line outside of East Room in Chicago’s Logan Square for Pachanga, a monthly dance party showcasing local Latino artists across all genres.

“A lot of people have been here. They keep saying, ‘Oh, you should come.’ Specifically, Pachanga night. Everyone says Pachanga Night is the one to come,” say Lopez. Familiar with popular reggaeton artists such as Ozuna, the pair are excited about a night of Latin music, despite not knowing the specific work of any of the DJs performing at this night’s Pachanga.

Inside the low-lit, cash-only venue, people danced to Drake’s voice playing over the cumbia beats. Others waited in line to get their face painted, Calavera-style, a decorative skull rooted in the Mexican tradition of El Día De Los Muertos, or The Day of The Dead. In the bar area, people showcase their elaborate costumes as they wait for the drink of the night, La Llorona, the East Room’s rendition of the classic Paloma. Plastered on the walls were posters promoting the November 3rd event, Xicago: a Redbull-sponsored event showcasing a range of Chicago-based Latin artists.

Since the rise of major hits like Luis Fonsi and Daddy Yankee’s “Despacito” and J Balvin’s “Mi Gente”, Latin music has established a stronger foothold in the American music mainstream. In 2017, a total of 19 Latin tracks placed on Billboard’s chart compared to only four Spanish songs in 2016 and three in 2015.

This is not the only way the music industry is evolving: popular streaming platforms like Spotify, SoundCloud and YouTube have allowed independent artists to more readily share their music, reaching listeners from all over the world. As of September 2018, Spotify began allowing artists to upload their music directly and bypass traditional digital distributors. For Latin indie artists, they now have more agency to embrace their identity and unique sound, without pressure to conform to the mainstream. For them, music is an avenue by which they can explore the intersection of their Latin heritage and their American upbringing.

Part of Chicago’s growing DIY music scene, four artists tell the stories of their “in-betweenness” as U.S. born Latinos: mixing music styles across cultures, singing in Spanglish and balancing the duality of their Latin-American identities.

Xicago was a celebration of local Latinx artists in Chicago. The event was one of many events thrown by Redbull’s Music Festival across Chicago. The artists showcased are pioneers in combining both their Latin and American influences into their music.

Inside the brick walls of what used to be a warehouse, Spanish, English and Spanglish bounced from one end to the other. Above the bar, colorful plastic pinwheels were hung upside down, leaving observers in a trance. Scattered across the two-stage venue were local Latinx owned business selling tacos de lengua and carne asada, empanadas, paletas, plants and clothing.

Social media is a crucial part of this changing landscape. Chicago-based artist Victor Cervantes, aka VICTOR!, grew up with the Internet boom and said, “Now, people don’t have to wait for you at shows to meet you in person. You can just go on [Instagram] Live and say hi,” said Cervantes.

Cervantes is candid on Instagram, posting anything from fleeting thoughts, childhood memories to pictures of himself cuddling dogs. It is a platform his followers can get to know not just his music as VICTOR!, but who he is: a first-generation Latino 18-year-old.

Cervantes is one of many Latinx artists taking part in Chicago’s rapidly evolving DIY music scene.

Dos Santos

The music of Dos Santos, a chicago-based quintet, fuses influences from Cumbia, Salsa, to classic rock and jazz. The group of five – Peter Vale, Alex Chavez, Daniel Villarreal-Carrillo, Jaime Garza and Nathan Karagianis – made their debut in May 2013, after lead vocalist Chavez moved to Chicago and found that there was a void of cumbia being performed.

“In the beginning, it was based in Cumbia. It was a psychedelic and aggressive sound,” Vale, the percussionist of Dos Santos, said. “But then, I’d say that the evolution of the group is more open and is a different sound. I know that many people have tried to classify what type of music we play, but it’s not possible.”

Beyond blending a range of styles, the music Dos Santos creates is in Spanglish because that is the language that is most natural to them.

“When Alex dropped the lyrics to ‘Return y Regreso,’ we didn’t even flinch to his lyrics and the fact that they were in English,” Vale said. “It was only when an interviewer pointed that out, which made me realize, ‘Woah that’s true! We have something in English!’ Other artists and bands are bilingual too. Down to the DJs who will play a Boogaloo song in English, it was an infectious Latin beat with English lyrics over the top. That kind of music is representative of Spanglish.”

Vale insisted that both his accent and the Spanish language are part of his Latin identity, as well as the Latin identity of Dos Santos.

“When I listen to ‘Hotel California’ from the Eagles, I am listening to them with Latino ears. When I hear something by Michael McDonald and want to do my own cover, it would have my own accent,” Vale said. “Being Latino, no matter what you do, is going to be perceived as having an accent. It’s about how well you’re going to embrace that. I embrace it fully. My music has an accent, and I am proud of that.”

La música de Dos Santos, dijo Vale, es una mezcla de Rock, de Cumbia o sonido latinoamericano.

"Con tiempo, tipo de Caribe, y de Afrobeat, es algo medio-oscuro y psicodélico, pero a la vez hay mucha consciencia. Eso es lo que es importante para nosotros: es que la música de nosotros diga algo y represente algo.”

Dos Santos' music, said Vale, is a mix of Rock, Cumbia, or a Latin American sound.

“Later on, some type of Caribbean and Afrobeat; it’s something half-dark and psychedelic, but at the same time it’s very conscientious. That’s something that’s important to us: that our music says something and represents something.”

Vale said that growing up, his mother owned an extensive vinyl collection, and he recalled listening to a lot of classic rock, like Michael McDonald and the Doobie Brothers, the Eagles and Kenny Loggins. Then there was also a lot of Salsa, like Frankie Ruiz.

“My mom put on these records, so I didn’t have a choice in what we were listening to. But it definitely shaped what I enjoy listening to,” Vale said. “The older Salsa recordings from the 70s would come on. My mom would grab me, and we’d start dancing in the living room. When you listen to music and you associate it to some kind of moment, like dancing with mom, how can you not fall in love with that music?”

La música de Dos Santos, un quinteto basado en Chicago, fusiona influencias de cumbia, salsa, rock y jazz. Los cinco miembros del grupo – Peter Vale, Alex Chavez, Daniel Villarreal-Carrillo, Jaime Garza and Nathan Karagianis – hicieron su debut en mayo 2013, después de Chavez se mudó a Chicago y se dio en cuenta de que no hay una presencia de cumbia.

“En el principio, estaba basado en la Cumbia. Era de un sonido psicodélico agresivo,” dijo Vale, el percusionista de Dos Santos. “ La evolución del grupo, yo diré que, está más abierto a diferentes países y a diferente sonidos. Yo sé que mucha personas han tratado de clasificar qué tipo de música nosotros tocamos, pero no es posible.”

Más allá de mezclar una gama de estilos, la música de Dos Santos es en Spanglish porque este idioma es la que más natural para ellos.

“Cuando Alex cantó las letras de ‘return y regreso,’ nunca nos echamos atrás a la hora del hecho de que las letras se impartieron en inglés,” dijo Vale. “Un entrevistador ha indicado el uso de Spanglish y solo no despertamos, ‘Esto es verdad! Tenemos algo de inglés!’ Otros artistas son bilingües también. Hay DJs que tocan música bugalú con letras en inglés. Esto es un ritmo latino infeccioso, y esto tipo de música es representativo de Spanglish.”

Vale dijo que su acento es un gran parte de su latinidad, al igual que la latinidad de Dos Santos.

“Cuando escucho a ‘Hotel California’ de the Eagles, estoy escuchando con orejas latinas. Cuando escucho algo de Michael McDonald y quiero hacer mi versión, tendría mi propio acento,” dijo Vale. “Para ser latino, hagamos lo que hagamos, será tener un acento. Se trata de cómo vas a abrazarlo. Yo lo abrazo totalmente. Mi música tiene un acento, y estoy orgulloso de ello.”

Además del acento, la música de Dos Santos, dijo Vale, es una mezcla de Rock, Cumbia o sonido latinoamericano.

“Con tiempo, tipo de Caribe, y de Afrobeat, es algo medio-oscuro y psicodélico, pero la ver mucho consciencia. Eso es lo que es importante para nosotros: es que la música de nosotros diga algo y represente algo.”

Vale dijo que cuando era niño, su madre es dueño de una colección extensiva de discos y recuerde escuchando el rock clásico, como Michael McDonald y the Doobie Brothers, the Eagles y Kenny Loggins. Había salsa también, como Frankie Ruiz.

“Mi mamá puso los discos, así que no tenía mucha opción de lo que escuchamos. Pero la música de mi crianza forma lo que disfruto escuchar hoy,” dijo Vale. “Cuando música de salsa que originaron en los años 70s se encendió, mi mamá me cogió y bailamos juntos en la sala de estar. La música que está asociada con una memoria, como bailando con mamá, cómo puedes no enamorarse de ello?”

Tatiana Hazel

First generation Mexican indie-pop singer Tatiana Hazel began building her following at the age of 13 through social media when she released her first original songs on YouTube. Hazel posted her first song in Spanish: a cover of Mexican indie-pop star Carla Morrison “Hasta La Piel.” While she loved the song, her comment section was filled strong criticism from her followers, who attacked her accent.

“I was so embarrassed. I speak it fluently, so I was like, ‘What?’” said Hazel.

From Missy Elliott to Slipknot, Hazel’s two older sisters exposed her to a diverse repertoire of American music at a young age. Despite not liking traditional Latin music her parents listened to, she was attracted to several indie-pop Mexican artists such as Carla Morrison, Julieta Venegas and Ximena Sariñana. These influences set the foundation for Hazel’s artistry.

“I didn’t think it was possible for me to do things in Spanish. They showed me the best of both worlds.” Hazel said.

Hazel’s first Spanish song, “Dímelo,” attracted one of the Latin Music Programmers at Apple Music, who messaged her on Instagram and had included her on several playlists. The following month, after playing a show in L.A, Hazel performed her EP “Toxic” to the Apple Music staff. After performing her music, she was also introduced to the Indie music programmer, who also added Hazel to certain playlists.

The 21-year-old artist and fashion designer continues to make music in Spanish, English and Spanglish, despite her critics. Her mixture of both languages pushes the boundaries of traditional music genres. When it comes to her songwriting process, language choice is not made intentional. Instead, her language choice is just a reflection of living in two cultures, a process that comes naturally to Hazel.

“I feel like some people might hear one song that I did in Spanish and think that's all I do,” she said. “Other people might know me for my whole EP, which is more like a pop, English sound. I think it just depends on what people listen to. Personally , I don’t consider myself one thing,” says Hazel.

La cantante de indie-pop Tatiana Hazel comenzó a desarrollar sus seguidores a la edad de 13 años a través de las redes sociales cuando subió sus primeras canciones originales en YouTube. Hazel publicó su primera canción en español: una portada de la estrella mexicana de indie-pop Carla Morrison "Hasta La Piel". Aunque le encantó la canción, su sección de comentarios recibió fuertes críticas de sus seguidores, que atacaron su acento.

"Estaba tan avergonzado. Lo hablo con fluidez, así que dije "¿Qué?", ​​dijo Hazel.

Desde Missy Elliott hasta Slipknot, las dos hermanas mayores de Hazel la expusieron a un repertorio diverso de música estadounidense a una edad temprana. A pesar de que no le gustaba la música latina tradicional que escuchaban sus padres, se sintió atraída por varios artistas indie-pop mexicanos como Carla Morrison, Julieta Venegas y Ximena Sariñana. Estas influencias establecen las bases para el arte de Hazel.

“No pensé que me fuera posible hacer cosas en español. Me mostraron lo mejor de ambos mundos ," dijo Hazel.

La primera canción en español de Hazel, "Dimelo", atrajo a uno de los programadores de música latina de Apple Music, quien le envió un mensaje a Instagram y la incluyó en varias listas de reproducción. El mes siguiente, después de tocar un concierto en L.A, Hazel tocó su EP "Toxic" para el personal de Apple Music. Después de presentar su música, también fue presentada al programador de música Indie, quien también agregó Hazel a ciertas listas de reproducción.

A pesar de sus críticos, la mexicana de primera generación continúa haciendo música en español, inglés y spanglish. Su mezcla de ambos idiomas empuja los límites de los géneros musicales tradicionales. Cuando se trata de su proceso de composición de canciones, la elección del idioma no se hace intencionalmente. En cambio, su elección de idioma es solo un reflejo de vivir en dos culturas, un proceso que es natural en Hazel.

"Siento que algunas personas pueden escuchar una canción que hice en español y pensar que eso es todo lo que hago", dijo. "Otras personas podrían conocerme por mi EP, que es más como un sonido pop en inglés. Creo que solo depende de lo que la gente escucha. Personalmente, no me considero una sola cosa ", dijo Hazel.

KAINA

Kaina Castillo, aka KAINA, grew up speaking Spanish, but like many first-generation children, she stopped feeling pressure from her parents to speak the language as she got older. Over time, she began to forget words.

Though many of her young Latinx music peers write songs in both English and Spanish, Castillo does not feel she can express herself equally in both languages.

“It almost feels like I’m appropriating something that is definitely already mine,” Castillo said of writing original music in Spanish, and instead favors incorporating Spanish covers into her live sets.

Castillo comes from a varied musical background. Raised in the Irving Park neighborhood of Chicago, she grew up listening to Motown records and Salsa, like Celia Cruz, at her parent’s parties. Though frequently categorized as soul or R&B, her songs also combine elements of music from her childhood. along with what she described a “softness and sweetness that came naturally as a songwriter.”

Though Castillo has been performing for six years as part of various projects, she released her first EP under the name KAINA in 2016: “sweet asl.,” a collaboration with fellow locals Bedows and the Burns Twins. She has since released a solo EP, “4u,” a track collaborating with Sen Morimoto, and her most recent August release, “f*cked up.”

KAINA blends her musical influences, but at times she feels uncomfortable with the space she occupies culturally as a relatively light-skinned, first-generation Latina growing up in Chicago.

“There are so many privileges I have and so many that I don’t,” Castillo said. “There’s this weird feeling of being mixed but you’re not.”, neither am I’m not.”

Castillo said that she has been able to explore her identity as a Latina and help her audience do the same. She described her main fan base as “really young Latinas,” and that fans often tell her that her shows helped them connect to their Latina identity in a way that they do not on a daily basis.

For Castillo, figuring out what it means for her to be a Latina artist is an ongoing process.

“Finding my identity as a Latina is going to be a continuous struggle,” Castillo said, “You can talk to anyone who makes music, who is Latinx, and their story is going to be completely different from mine, and that’s part of it.”

Kaina Castillo, alias KAINA, crecía hablando español, pero como muchos niños de primera generación, cuando ella se ponía mayor sus padres dejaban de insistir que ella aprendiera la idioma y ella empezaba a olvidar palabras.

Aunque muchas de sus compañeras jóvenes de música Latinx escriben canciones en inglés y español, Castillo no siente que puede expresarse lo mismo en ambos idiomas.

“Casi siente como me estoy apropiando algo que es definitivamente ya la mía,” dice Castillo sobre escribiendo música original en español, y en vez favorece incorporando covers españolas en sus conciertos en vivo.

Castillo viene de un fondo musical variado. Criado en el barrio de Irving Park en Chicago, Castillo crecí escuchando a tapas de Motown y música Salsa como Celia Cruz a las fiestas de sus padres. Aunque ella está categorizado con frecuencia como música Soul o R&B, sus canciones también mezclan elementos de su fondo musical junto con un “suavidad y dulzor que viene naturalmente como compositor.”

Aunque Castillo ha cantando con varias bandas para seis años, ella lanzó su primer EP bajo el nombre KAINA en 2016: “sweet asl.”, un colaboración con compañeros locales Bedows y los Burns Twins. Desde esta tapa, ella ha lanzado un EP solitario, “4u”, un canción que colabora con Sen Morimoto, y su lanzamiento más reciente de agosto, “f*cked up.”

KAINA es adepta a mezclando sus influencias musicales, pero a veces ella siente incómodo con el espacio ella ocupa culturalmente como una Latina de piel relativamente clara, de generación primera viviendo en Chicago. “Hay tantos privilegios que tengo y tantos que no,” dice Castillo. “Hay una sensación raro de estar mezclado pero no soy, porque no soy.”

Castillo dice que ha sido capaz de explorar su identidad Latina y ayudar su audiencia en hacer lo mismo: ella describe su fan base principal como “Latinas muy jóvenes,” y dice que sus fans le dicen con frecuencia que ellas conciertos les ayuden a conectar a sus identidades Latinas en una manera que no hacen diariamente. Castillo es un representación encarnada de qué significa ser un artista Latina y jóven.

Para Castillo, averiguar qué significa ser un artista Latina es un proceso en curso. “Averiguando mi identidad como Latina sería una lucha continua,” dice Castillo, “Porque, puedes hablar con cualquier persona Latinx que hace música, y su historia va a ser completamente diferente a la mía, y esto es parte de ella.”

VICTOR!

Cervantes likens his music to a thick blanket.

“When you go washing and you just dried all of your clothes, it smells like softener,” the singer says. “You take your blanket out of the drier and wrap yourself in it. That’s how my music kind of feels.”

While many 18-year-olds are completing their college applications, Cervantes spends his days recording music and making beats. The Mexican-American artist grew up in the city-encapsulated suburb of Cicero, Illinois. He was first exposed to music through singing at church, and shortly after, making church music turned into making music for himself and his friends.

“I was writing songs for my youth group, but then I felt that was kind of boring,” says Cervantes. “I started writing my own stuff, and since I already knew how to make beats, I started making my own songs,” says Cervantes.

Cervantes grew up listening to Eydie Gormé, Los Panchos and the Mexican rock band Maná. Cervantes said he is grateful for the growing interest in Latin music in the U.S. and while some worry this national fixation could lead to cultural appropriation, he sees as an opportunity for Latinos to shape how the U.S. perceives them.

The increased attention directed towards Latinx artists gives them a platform and a voice, which could increase understanding of the Latinx community.

As a first-generation Mexican living in the U.S., Cervantes said it is difficult to embrace his culture through language. When growing up around white friends, Cervantes was careful to never speak Spanish, scared of what they would think. However, as he grew older and began to embrace his Latin identity, he felt more confident exploring the language through music, incorporating lyrics in both Spanish and English into his more recent songs.

“It has become easier for artists like me, Omar Apollo and Cuco to start singing in Spanish. We feel more comfortable in our own skin,” Cervantes says. “People are seeing this as a new era of music.”

Victor Cervantes, alias VICTOR!, compara su música a una manta gruesa.

"Cuando estás lavando la ropa, y acabas de secar todo, huele a suavizante,” dice el cantante. “La sacas del secador, y te envuelves en la manta.”

Cuando otros adolescentes de la edad de dieciocho están terminando las solicitudes para asistir la universidad, Cervantes pasa los días grabando música y haciendo ritmos para sí mismo y sus amigos.

“Estaba escribiendo canciones para mi grupo de jóvenes, pero me sentía que era un poco aburrido,” dice Cervantes. “Empecé a escribir mis propias cosas, y porque ya sé componer los ritmos, empecé a escribir mis propias canciones,” dice Cervantes.

Cervantes se crió escuchando la música de Eydie Gormé, Los Panchos y la banda mexicana de rock, Maná. Cervantes dice que está agradecido para el interés creciente en la música latina en los EEUU, y mientras otros se preocupan que esta fijación nacional puede resultar en la comodificación cultural, él ve una oportunidad para que los latinos puedan formar cómo los EEUU les percibe.

La atención aumentada dirigida hacia las artistas latinas les da un plataforma y una voz, el cual puede incrementar el entendimiento de la comunidad latina.

Como un mexicano de la primera generación que vive en los EEUU, Cervantes dice que es difícil abrazar su cultura por el idioma. Se crió rodeado por amigos blancos, Cervantes tenía cuidado de nunca hablar el español por el miedo de lo que pensarían sus amigos. Sin embargo, cuando era mayor, empezó a abrazar su latinidad, y se sentía confidente explorando el idioma por la música, incorporando la letra en los dos español y inglés en canciones más recientes.

“Ha llegado a ser más fácil empezar a cantar en español para artistas como yo, Omar Apollo y Cuco. Nos sentimos más cómodos en nuestra propia piel,” Cervantes dice. “A otras personas les parece que esta es una nueva época de música.”
